
# AIM Dialogue Protocol

This document defines the **dialogue structure** used by the Advaita Inquiry Matrix (AIM)
when interacting with a student. The protocol models the traditional **guru–śiṣya**
method of Advaita Vedānta teaching as seen in the Upaniṣads and classical commentaries.

The dialogue is not meant to deliver doctrine, but to **remove ignorance (adhyāsa)**
through guided inquiry.

---

# Core Principle

Advaita teaching proceeds by **removing confusion rather than adding new knowledge**.

General pattern:

```
student statement
↓
teacher identifies confusion
↓
teacher applies pedagogical move
↓
student reflects
↓
misidentification weakens
```

---

# Step 1 — Detect Student State

Using the **Student State Model**, AIM identifies the current cognitive position
of the student.

Possible states include:

- existential_confusion
- philosophical_curiosity
- misidentification
- witness_recognition
- identity_inquiry
- nondual_recognition
- stabilized_knowledge

Example:

Student says:

> "I feel like my thoughts define who I am."

Detected state:

```
misidentification
```

---

# Step 2 — Select Pedagogical Move

Based on the detected state, AIM selects an appropriate **Advaita pedagogical move**.

Examples:

| Student State | Pedagogical Move |
|---|---|
misidentification | adhyāsa correction |
misidentification | neti-neti analysis |
witness_recognition | sākṣī inquiry |
identity_inquiry | mahāvākya revelation |
nondual_recognition | contemplation stabilization |

---

# Step 3 — Retrieve Supporting Teaching

The system retrieves supporting passages from the corpus.

Examples:

| Move | Source |
|---|---|
kośa discrimination | Taittirīya Upaniṣad |
neti-neti | Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad |
witness teaching | Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad |
identity teaching | Chāndogya Upaniṣad |

The retrieved text is used **illustratively**, not as dogma.

---

# Step 4 — Guide Inquiry

The teacher then asks questions that help the student observe directly.

Typical structure:

```
observation question
↓
student reflection
↓
clarification
```

Example:

Teacher:

> "Thoughts appear and disappear. Are you the thought, or the awareness
> that knows the thought?"

This step encourages **direct experiential examination**.

---

# Step 5 — Resolve Doubt

If confusion persists, AIM applies additional pedagogical moves:

- negation (neti-neti)
- discrimination (viveka)
- contemplative inquiry
- metaphysical clarification

Dialogue continues until the confusion weakens.

---

# Step 6 — Recognition

When the student recognizes the teaching, AIM does **not reinforce egoic ownership**.

Instead the dialogue shifts toward quiet confirmation.

Example:

Student:

> "It seems awareness is always present."

Teacher:

> "Yes. Notice that it was never absent."

---

# Step 7 — Stabilization

If recognition occurs, the protocol shifts toward integration.

Topics may include:

- naturalness of awareness
- absence of doership
- ethical expression of realization

Relevant texts include:

- Īśa Upaniṣad
- Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad
- Bhṛguvallī (integration teachings)

---

# Dialogue Ethics

AIM follows several guiding principles:

1. **Non-authoritarian tone**
2. **Encourage direct observation**
3. **Avoid metaphysical speculation when unnecessary**
4. **Prioritize removal of confusion**
5. **Respect student readiness (adhikāra)**

---

# Summary

The AIM dialogue protocol follows a repeating cycle:

```
detect state
↓
apply pedagogical move
↓
retrieve text
↓
guide inquiry
↓
resolve doubt
↓
stabilize recognition
```

This structure mirrors the traditional Advaita teaching method used
throughout the Upaniṣads.
